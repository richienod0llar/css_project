How this project was built

This project exists to make a long, qualitative debate about fashion‚Äôs ‚Äúmood‚Äù measurable. By quantifying color across decades of runway imagery, we can test whether cultural narratives (like minimalism after the early 1990s) show up in the data.

Started with the Vogue Runway image dataset, a separate metadata table was created with 3 datasets cleaned, de-duplicated and merged together so every photo had a year, season, designer, and collection type. Images were resized to 256√ó256 to standardize sampling and reduce compute without losing palette structure.

The core algorithm extracts dominant colors using K-Means (k=6) in LAB color space, which aligns with human perception better than RGB. For each image, the cluster centers become its palette; lightness (L*), chroma, and color diversity (cluster spread) are computed to capture both intensity and variety.

To compare across years, each image is matched to one of 37 Sanzo Wada palettes. The match uses ŒîE 2000 (CIEDE2000) to find the closest palette by perceptual distance, creating a consistent ‚Äúvocabulary‚Äù of colors for time-series analysis.

Finally, these metrics are aggregated by year, decade, designer, and season, validate the trends for outliers, and translate the results into D3 visualizations.

Data Source

30,000 images from Vogue Runway (1988-2023), spanning 1,549 designers across Ready-to-Wear, Couture, and Menswear collections.

Color Extraction

K-Means clustering (k=6) in LAB color space to identify dominant colors. Images resized to 256√ó256 pixels for efficiency.

Palette Mapping

Each image matched to 37 unique Sanzo Wada palettes using ŒîE 2000 (CIEDE2000), a perceptually uniform color difference metric.

Analysis

Temporal trends calculated using weighted averages. Lightness (L*: 0-255), Saturation (Chroma), and Diversity (color cluster std. dev.) computed for each image.

What do 30,000 runway photos from top brands reveal about fashion?

Over nearly four decades, from 1988 to 2023, fashion underwent a dramatic transformation. Not in silhouettes. Not in hemlines. But in something more subtle, yet profound: color.

This is an analysis of 30,000 images from Vogue Runway, spanning 1,549 designers across 36 years, using perceptually accurate color science from Sanzo Wada's 1933 "Dictionary of Color Combinations" to map the evolution of fashion's chromatic mood.

The 1980s: Bold and Dark

The analysis begins in 1989, whenfashion was characterized by darker, bolder palettes. The mean lightness value was just 126, reflecting the power dressing and aesthetics of the era.

The 2000s: Rising Lightness

Entering the new decade, we see that fashion began to lighten. By 2009, the mean lightness had climbed to 140, representing a shift toward softer, more ethereal aesthetics.

The 2010s: Peak Minimalism

The 2010s saw fashion at its lightest, with designers favoring whites, creams, and pale neutrals. This was the era of "normcore" and quiet luxury.

Today: Stable Minimalism

In the 2020s, lightness has stabilized around 132, suggesting that fashion has found a new equilibrium in subdued, sophisticated palettes.

But it's not just about light and dark

Perhaps even more dramatic than the shift in lightness was the desaturation of fashion.

Measuring the shift

Using the ŒîE 2000 metric: a perceptually accurate measure of color difference, we can quantify how much fashion's color palette changed decade to decade.

1980s ‚Üí 1990s: The Biggest Jump

The transition from the 1980s to 1990s showed a color distance of 3.97: the largest shift in our dataset. Fashion was transforming rapidly.

1990s ‚Üí 2000s: Still Changing

The next decade brought another significant shift of 3.27. Fashion was still searching for its new identity.

2000s ‚Üí 2010s: Stabilization

Then something changed. The color distance dropped to just 1.24, a clear sign designers had found their new aesthetic.

2010s ‚Üí 2020s: The New Normal

The 2020s show the smallest shift yet: 0.54. For over 15 years, fashion has maintained a remarkably consistent color palette.

Color shifts after the Cold War

The largest chromatic jump, from the saturated, neon-heavy 1980s into the muted ‚Äô90s, lines up with a seismic political reset. The fall of the Berlin Wall (1989), German reunification, and the collapse of the Soviet Union (1991) opened fashion to a more global, minimalist sensibility. Designers traded bold, ideological statements for quieter palettes that resonated with this newfound international optimism.

By the end of the 1990s, color distance remained elevated as collections rethought identity for an integrated world; the 4.44/3.27 swings show designers still searching for a new narrative. After 2001 the runway shifted darker and denser, with mean lightness falling from ~130 to ~115 in a single year while saturation climbed, capturing a post-9/11, post-industrial mood that felt defensive rather than celebratory.

Post-2005 the data finally settle: ŒîE drops to 1.24 and then 0.54, and annual lightness/saturation hover around ~132 and ~11. That plateau correlates with the rise of ‚Äúquiet luxury,‚Äù sustainability mandates, and digital-first retail, which encouraged designers to keep palettes restrained and consistent instead of chasing dramatic swings.

Designer signatures persist

Even in an era of color convergence, individual designers maintain distinct chromatic identities.

What goes around, comes around

But fashion is not just a linear progression. It's cyclical. Trends from past decades resurface, reimagined but recognizable.

The "20-year rule" of fashion suggests that styles return approximately two decades later. Our data confirms this pattern in color choices.

Decade-to-Decade Palette Overlap

Which palettes from past decades returned in later years?

The browns, grays, and muted tones that defined 90s minimalism, think Calvin Klein and Jil Sander have returned as the foundation of contemporary "quiet luxury." Fashion has come full circle.

The minimalist consensus

Fashion moves in cycles, but the current era of light, desaturated, neutral palettes has shown remarkable staying power.

This isn't just a trend that will cycle out in a few years. It's a fundamental shift in how luxury presents itself.

From the bold, saturated colors of the 1980s to the "quiet luxury" of today, fashion's chromatic evolution mirrors broader cultural shifts toward restraint, sophistication, and understated wealth.
